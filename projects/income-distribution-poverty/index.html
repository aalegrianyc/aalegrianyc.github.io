Income Distribution and Poverty Metrics

Hi! Below are two interactive spreadsheets and the charts I made with them showcasing different analyses.

Definitions: Money-Income refers to the total income received by a household, including wages, salaries, benefits, and other cash earnings, without adjustments for household size or composition. On the other hand, Equivalence-Adjusted Income takes into account household size and composition, adjusting the income measure to reflect the different needs of households.

Summary: My aim was to gain a little bit more of a better understanding of the relationship between income inequality and household size or needs (reflected by equivalence adjustments) by examining each quintile and the top 5 percent of earners. In the second part of this project, I just wanted a preliminary look at poverty metrics as they pertained to race. The poverty rates show notable disparities that have persisted post-COVID, though the analysis suggests that racial/ethnic differences in poverty rates are statistically significant based on the margin of error.

i. Income Distribution using Money and Equivalence-Adjusted Income

The first is Table A-3 from the Census' data on income. Using the data, I created a clustered bar plot showing the share of aggregate income for each quintile and the top 5 percent for 2021 and 2022 using Money-Income. Then I created a bar plot showing the same using Equivalence Adjusted Income. I wanted to see the difference between the two years and the two types of income.

There are very slight differences between 2021 and 2022 estimates. Similarly, there are very slight differences between money income and equivalence-adjusted income estimates. It doesn't appear that the estimates reveal any statistically significant change from year to year or depending on which measure is used when accounting for margin of error. The sobering estimates reveal a large income disparity: are that the highest quintile owns about 50% of the wealth in the US, with the top 5% owning 20% and the lowest owning about 3%.

ii. Poverty Rates by Race and Hispanic Origin

This is Table A-1 from the Census' data on poverty. I created a bar plot comparing poverty rates (percent in poverty) by Race and Hispanic Origin for 2022, including error bars based on the reported margins of error. I was looking to see if the differences in poverty rates seemed statistically significant.

But without a rigorous method to have accurately determined if the differences are statistically significant, I intuited that they are because the margin of error for each rate is small relatively with a 90% CI.
